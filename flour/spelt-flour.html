Spelt Flour

What Is It?

Spelt (also called dinkel wheat, or Triticum spelta) is an ancient relative of modern wheat — cultivated for thousands of years before modern bread wheat took over. It fell out of mainstream use during the 20th century but has seen a real revival among artisan bakers over the last two decades, mostly because of its flavor. Spelt has a nuttiness and sweetness that's genuinely hard to replicate with white flour. A 20% spelt blend turns a good country loaf into a noticeably better one.

The catch is the gluten. Spelt has sufficient protein to form a gluten network, but that network is fundamentally different from wheat's — it's extensible rather than elastic. Wheat gluten stretches and snaps back. Spelt gluten stretches and keeps stretching. In practical terms, this means it's more susceptible to over-development: if you work the dough too hard, mix too long, or fold too aggressively, the gluten breaks down and the dough becomes slack, sticky, and impossible to shape. The loaf spreads instead of rising.

⚠ The single most common spelt mistake: treating it like bread flour. It needs fewer folds, gentler shaping, and a cooler dough temperature. Work it too hard and you'll be making flatbread.

The Flavor Case

The reason bakers bother with spelt's handling difficulties is the flavor. Spelt has a distinctive nuttiness — not aggressive, not overwhelming, but clearly present in every bite. Combined with sourdough's natural tang, the result is a loaf with real complexity: sour on the finish, wheaty in the middle, nutty on the crust. It's the flour that makes people ask what you did differently.

White spelt (sifted to remove most of the bran) has a milder flavor but is easier to work with — it behaves somewhat closer to bread flour while retaining much of the flavor character. Whole spelt amplifies both the flavor and the handling challenges. For most home bakers, white or light spelt at 20–30% is the best entry point.

Hydration: Less Than You'd Think

Spelt absorbs water differently than wheat. It hydrates faster — the dough will feel workable sooner — but it can also feel wetter than it is, which leads bakers to add more water than needed. The result is a dough that's too slack by the end of bulk fermentation.

Rule: reduce hydration by 3–5% when adding spelt, not increase it. A recipe at 78% with 100% bread flour should move to roughly 73–75% when 25% is swapped to spelt. The extensible gluten can't hold as much water in the structure, and the dough needs to be firmer to maintain shape during the cold proof.

Blend Guide

% Spelt Flavor Effect Handling Hydration Adjustment
10% Subtle nuttiness, slightly rounder finish Minimal change — most bakers won't notice Reduce hydration -1 to -2%
20% Clearly nutty, sweet finish, great crust color Slightly extensible. Reduce folds to 3 sets. Reduce hydration -3 to -4%
30% Pronounced spelt flavor — noticeably different loaf Handle gently. Cold fermentation recommended. Reduce hydration -5 to -6%
50%+ Dominant spelt flavor Requires substantial technique adjustment — treat as a different dough type Significantly lower baseline. Work cold.
Cold proofing (overnight in the fridge) is particularly good for spelt-heavy doughs. The cold firms up the extensible gluten, gives you a clean scoring surface, and slows things down enough that minor overproofing is less catastrophic.
